## Executive Summary

**Overall Risk Rating: MODERATE — Proceed with Conditions**

The proposed mark **CHARTERWELL** for use in connection with AI-powered document processing software for insurance, legal, and document-heavy industries presents **manageable but non-trivial trademark risk**. No identical registered mark exists for "CHARTERWELL" in any searched jurisdiction. However, multiple live "CHARTWELL" marks and common-law users operate in adjacent financial services, insurance, and technology spaces, creating a meaningful likelihood-of-confusion landscape that must be actively managed.

The two highest-severity conflicts are:

1. **Chartwell Insurance Services (now EPIC)** — Active insurance brokerage using "CHARTWELL" in the insurance industry in the US
2. **Chartwell Compliance (now Ankura)** — Fintech compliance advisory using "CHARTWELL" in financial technology/regulatory compliance

This opinion recommends **proceeding with registration** in targeted classes, with specific mitigation steps to reduce opposition risk and build a defensible portfolio. Renaming is not required at this stage, but the board should understand the litigation exposure before committing post-launch brand equity.

## 3. DuPont Likelihood-of-Confusion Analysis

| **2. Similarity of goods/services** | Moderate-High — Our client's software serves insurance industry; Chartwell IS insurance services. Not identical goods, but the same customers encounter both. | Moderate factor for confusion |
| **3. Similarity of trade channels** | Moderate — Both target insurance industry participants, though at different points (software vendor vs. brokerage). | Moderate factor for confusion |

## 5. Registrability Assessment

### 5.1 Distinctiveness

"CHARTERWELL" is a coined term — it does not appear in standard dictionaries and is not descriptive of any goods or services. This is favorable for registration. The mark would be classified as **suggestive to arbitrary** for AI-powered document processing software, which supports registrability.

The "CHARTER" element may be considered suggestive of legal/regulatory documents (e.g., corporate charters), but the composite "CHARTERWELL" does not directly describe the goods/services.

### 5.2 Section 2(d) Refusal Risk

The primary registrability concern is a Section 2(d) likelihood-of-confusion refusal based on the existing CHARTWELL marks. The examining attorney may cite:

- **CHARTWELL AI / CHARTWELLAI** (Serial Nos. 99558440, 99558514) — Class 42 SaaS overlap
- **CHARTWELL** (Reg. No. 4,113,686) — Chartwell Retirement; Class 43 (likely distinguishable)
- **CHARTWELL RETIREMENT RESIDENCES** (Reg. No. 4,834,331) — Class 36/37 (arguable overlap in Class 36)

**Assessment:** A Section 2(d) Office Action citing ChartWellAI is probable for a Class 42 filing. Charterwell should be prepared to file a response distinguishing the goods/services (insurance/legal document processing vs. healthcare clinical workflow) and arguing that the crowded field of CHARTWELL marks dilutes any single mark's zone of exclusion.

### 5.3 Section 2(e) Descriptiveness Risk

Low. "CHARTERWELL" is not descriptive of software, AI, document processing, or insurance services.

## 7. Conclusion & Board-Level Summary

**Can we proceed with CHARTERWELL?** Yes — with conditions.

**What are the conditions?**

1. File in Classes 9, 35, and 42 (not Class 36 initially)
2. Secure domains and social media handles immediately
3. Budget for a potential Office Action response citing ChartWellAI (~$2,500-$5,000 in attorney fees)
4. Set up trademark monitoring to track ChartWellAI and Chartwell Compliance developments
5. Be prepared for a possible coexistence negotiation with EPIC if they notice the mark

**What would change this recommendation?**

- If Chartwell Insurance/EPIC files a standalone "CHARTWELL" mark in Class 36 or 42 — risk escalates to HIGH
- If ChartWellAI's applications register and they broaden their goods/services description — risk escalates
- If the product pivots to directly underwriting or selling insurance (Class 36) — risk escalates significantly

**What is the worst-case scenario?**

EPIC (backed by significant resources) files an opposition after publication. This would cost $15,000-$50,000+ to defend and take 12-24 months. The outcome would depend on whether the TTAB finds the goods/services sufficiently related. This is a manageable but real risk.

**What is the cost of renaming?**

Pre-launch: Painful but feasible (new domains, updated materials, legal fees). Estimated $25,000-$75,000 depending on stage.
Post-launch: Catastrophic. Rebranding after market recognition costs $500,000+ and destroys SEO/brand equity built to date.

**Bottom line:** The name CHARTERWELL is registrable and defensible, but it is not a clean clearance. The board should proceed with eyes open, funded for enforcement, and with a monitoring program in place from day one.

**Nine active entities** using "Chartwell" or "Charterwell" across multiple unrelated industries demonstrates that "CHARTWELL" is not a strong, distinctive mark owned by any single entity — it is a **crowded field** where consumers and purchasers are accustomed to distinguishing among multiple "Chartwell"-branded businesses.

Federal courts and the TTAB have consistently held that marks in a crowded field are entitled to a narrower scope of protection, and that newcomers in unrelated goods/services can coexist where subtle differences exist. The addition of the "ER" in CHARTERWELL — while minimal — combined with entirely different goods/services (AI document processing software vs. insurance brokerage, investment management, retirement homes, healthcare AI, etc.) provides a defensible position.

**Caveat:** The crowded field doctrine strengthens the defense but does not guarantee success. It reduces the zone of exclusion for each prior mark but does not eliminate it entirely. A crowded field argument is most effective when the applicant's goods/services are genuinely distinct from the cited mark's goods/services — which is the case here for all identified conflicts.
