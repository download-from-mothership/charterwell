# Charterwell Buyer Persona Research

**Prepared by:** CMO | **Date:** 2026-03-21 | **Issue:** KEN-38

---

## Executive Summary

Enterprise insurance sales involve a buying committee, not a single decision-maker. Charterwell must navigate four distinct personas — each with different pain points, success metrics, and objections. This document profiles each persona in depth to inform messaging, content strategy, sales enablement, and product positioning.

The personas map to the classic enterprise buying committee:

| Role | Persona | Buying Function |
|---|---|---|
| VP/SVP Claims Operations | **The Operator** | Primary buyer — owns the budget and the pain |
| Claims Team Lead / Senior Adjuster | **The Champion** | Internal advocate — lives in the product daily |
| CIO / Chief Digital Officer | **The Architect** | Economic buyer — controls technology spend and architecture |
| Chief Compliance Officer | **The Gatekeeper** | Blocker — can kill a deal with a single "no" |

---

## Persona 1: The Operator

### VP / SVP of Claims Operations

**Demographics:** 45-58 years old. 20+ years in insurance, most spent climbing from adjuster to management. Typically at a mid-to-large P&C carrier ($1B-$20B GWP). Reports to the COO or CEO. Manages 50-500+ claims professionals.

---

### Top 3 Pain Points

**1. Speed of Claims Resolution — The Cycle Time Trap**

The average P&C claim still touches 10-15 documents across 17 data sources. Examiners spend 15-20 minutes per FNOL just validating information that should be obvious. Every day a claim stays open costs money — in reserves, in adjuster labor, in customer dissatisfaction. The Operator is under constant pressure to reduce cycle time without sacrificing accuracy.

The 2026 reality: catastrophe losses are intensifying (climate-driven events are more frequent and severe), creating surge workloads that overwhelm existing staff. Crawford's 2026 outlook identifies this as the dominant force shaping claims — carriers need the ability to scale throughput without proportional headcount growth.

**2. Adjuster Productivity — The Talent Crisis**

Experienced adjusters are retiring. New hires lack institutional knowledge and take 12-18 months to reach full productivity. The Operator can't hire fast enough, can't train fast enough, and can't afford the error rates from inexperienced staff handling complex claims.

Meanwhile, adjusters are "inundated with information" from multiple disconnected tools — predictive analytics outputs, damage estimates, policy data, medical records — and the challenge is managing their time and evaluating all of it effectively. The Operator needs technology that makes every adjuster perform like a 10-year veteran.

**3. Claims Leakage and Fraud Loss**

Claims leakage — preventable overspend from missed subrogation, duplicate payments, overpayments, and incomplete policy checks — silently erodes profitability. Combined with an estimated $80B+ in annual U.S. insurance fraud, the Operator is bleeding money they can't always see. Point solutions catch some of it (Shift flags fraud; subrogation tools catch recovery opportunities), but no single system connects the dots across the full claim lifecycle.

---

### Metrics They Report to the C-Suite

| Metric | What It Measures | 2026 Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| **Combined ratio** | Overall underwriting profitability (loss ratio + expense ratio) | Industry avg: ~96.9 (AM Best forecast) |
| **Loss adjustment expense (LAE) ratio** | Cost to settle claims as % of earned premium | Target: <12% for well-run operations |
| **Claims cycle time** | Average days from FNOL to resolution | Varies by line; auto ~30 days, complex liability 6-18 months |
| **Customer satisfaction / NPS** | Policyholder experience during claims | Top quartile carriers target NPS >50 |
| **Claims leakage rate** | Preventable overspend as % of total claims paid | Industry avg: 5-10% of total indemnity spend |
| **Touches per claim** | Number of human interactions to resolve a claim | Lower is better; benchmark varies by complexity |

**The pitch that lands:** "Cut your claims cycle time in half without adding headcount — and see where the leakage is hiding."

---

### How They Evaluate Technology

- **Proof over promise.** They've been burned by vendor demos that don't match production reality. They want to see the product work on *their* documents, *their* claim types, *their* edge cases.
- **Pilot-first.** They won't sign an enterprise deal without a 60-90 day pilot on a contained book of business. Success criteria must be defined upfront: X% reduction in cycle time, Y% improvement in extraction accuracy, Z% reduction in touches per claim.
- **Operational impact, not technical sophistication.** They don't care about model architectures or training data. They care about: will my adjusters use it? Will it break my existing workflows? How fast can we go live?
- **Change management cost.** The hidden objection: "My team is already exhausted. I can't ask them to learn another tool." Charterwell's workspace framing directly addresses this — we replace tools, not add to the stack.

---

### Objections and How We Address Them

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "We already have tools for this." | "You have 10+ tools that don't talk to each other. That's not a solution, it's a new problem. One workspace replaces the stack." |
| "My team can't handle another implementation." | "Charterwell is designed to replace complexity, not add to it. We start with your highest-pain document workflow and expand from there." |
| "What if the AI makes a mistake on a claim?" | "Human oversight is built in. Charterwell surfaces recommendations and flags anomalies — your adjusters make the decisions. We augment expertise, we don't replace it." |
| "I need to see ROI in 90 days." | "That's exactly what the pilot is for. We define success metrics together upfront and measure against them weekly." |

## Persona 2: The Champion

### Claims Team Lead / Senior Adjuster

**Demographics:** 30-45 years old. 5-15 years in claims. Tech-forward — they were the first on their team to adopt new tools. Frustrated with the current tech stack but pragmatic about what's possible. They don't control the budget, but they control adoption.

---

### Why This Persona Matters

The Champion is the person who will:
1. **Discover** Charterwell (organic search, conference demo, peer recommendation)
2. **Advocate** internally ("I found something that could fix our document problem")
3. **Pilot** the product (they'll be the power user who proves value)
4. **Drive adoption** across the team (if they love it, their team follows)

Without a Champion, enterprise deals stall. The VP might approve the budget, but the team lead determines whether the product gets used.

---

### Their Daily Reality

- Opens 30-50 claims per day across multiple systems
- Alt-tabs between claims management system (Guidewire/Duck Creek), document viewer, email, spreadsheets, and 3-4 other tools
- Manually reviews FNOL reports, police reports, medical records, and repair estimates — copying data between systems
- Spends 40-60% of their time on administrative tasks rather than claim evaluation
- Mentors junior adjusters who ask the same questions repeatedly because institutional knowledge isn't systematized
- Gets measured on throughput (claims closed) but cares about accuracy (getting the right outcome)

---

### Top Pain Points

**1. Tool Fragmentation**
"I have 8 tabs open right now and none of them talk to each other." The fragmented technology experience — different tools and vendors for different parts of the claims process — is the single biggest daily frustration. Data is inconsistent, incomplete, or siloed across systems.

**2. Document Drudgery**
Reviewing, extracting, and cross-referencing claim documents is the most time-consuming part of the job. Medical records alone can be hundreds of pages. The Champion knows there's a better way but hasn't found a tool that actually understands insurance documents (not just OCR that spits out raw text).

**3. Training Burden**
As the most experienced person on the team, they're constantly training junior adjusters. They wish they could encode their expertise into a system so new hires could get up to speed faster.

---

### How They Evaluate Technology

- **Hands-on trial.** They want to use the product themselves, on real claims, before recommending it. A slick demo isn't enough.
- **Speed to value.** "Can I see a difference in my first week?" They don't have patience for 6-month implementations.
- **Integration pragmatism.** They know the IT team will push back on anything that requires a massive integration project. They favor tools that can start standalone and integrate later.
- **Peer validation.** They trust other claims professionals more than vendor marketing. Online reviews, conference hallway conversations, and LinkedIn posts from peers carry enormous weight.

## Persona 3: The Architect

### CIO / Chief Digital Officer

**Demographics:** 45-55 years old. Technology background, often from outside insurance (banking, healthcare, consulting). 3-7 years at current carrier, hired specifically to drive digital transformation. Reports to CEO. Manages a technology budget of $10M-$200M+ depending on carrier size.

---

### 2026 Priorities

Based on Gartner's 2026 CIO Agenda, PwC's 2026 CIO outlook, and insurance-specific research:

**1. From AI Experiments to AI ROI**

The honeymoon with AI pilots is over. CIOs are under pressure to show clear cause-and-effect: AI initiative → measurable business outcome. The Insurance Edge captured it perfectly: the 2026 CIO agenda is "from co-pilot chaos to clear AI ROI." Prediction engines must be paired with platforms that drive action — workflow automation, intelligent routing, triggered decisions. AI that generates insights but doesn't change operations is no longer funded.

**2. Legacy System Modernization**

68% of insurance companies are upgrading core systems to cloud-based platforms, and 41% of CIOs identify legacy systems as the primary obstacle to digital transformation. But rip-and-replace is too risky and expensive for most carriers. CIOs want a "surround and extend" strategy: new AI-native tools that sit alongside (and eventually replace) legacy cores, extracting value without a $50M re-platforming project.

**3. AI Governance by Design**

In regulated industries, AI governance isn't optional. In 2026, CIOs must build guardrails into every intelligent system from the start — not retrofit rules after deployment. This means: explainable decisions, audit trails, model monitoring, bias detection, and data lineage. Any vendor that can't demonstrate governance capabilities is disqualified.

**4. Vendor Consolidation**

CIOs are tired of managing 50+ vendor relationships in their technology stack. They want fewer, deeper partnerships with vendors who can expand across use cases. The "workspace" framing directly appeals to this: one vendor, one platform, multiple capabilities.

---

### What Proof Points They Need

| Proof Point | Why It Matters | How Charterwell Delivers |
|---|---|---|
| **ROI model** | Must justify spend to CFO and board | Pilot metrics → projected savings at scale. Per-claim cost reduction model. |
| **Security certifications** | Non-negotiable for enterprise procurement | SOC 2 Type II, data encryption at rest/transit, role-based access, tenant isolation |
| **Integration architecture** | Must fit existing tech stack | API-first design. Pre-built connectors for Guidewire, Duck Creek, and major CMS platforms. |
| **AI governance** | Regulatory compliance and board reporting | Explainable AI outputs, decision audit trails, model performance dashboards, bias monitoring |
| **Scalability** | Must handle enterprise volume | Cloud-native architecture (Cloudflare edge), horizontal scaling, SLA guarantees |
| **Data residency** | Regulatory and policy requirements | Configurable data residency, no cross-tenant data sharing, data deletion on request |

---

### How They Evaluate Technology

- **Architecture review.** They (or their enterprise architecture team) will scrutinize the technical design: API design, data model, security posture, deployment model, scalability characteristics.
- **Vendor viability.** Is this company going to exist in 3 years? They want to understand funding, team, and roadmap.
- **Total cost of ownership.** License cost is just the start. They factor in integration cost, training cost, change management cost, and ongoing maintenance.
- **Reference customers.** They will call other CIOs who've deployed the product. No references = no deal at enterprise scale.

---

### Objections and How We Address Them

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "We're mid-migration to Guidewire Cloud. I can't add another platform." | "Charterwell sits on top of your CMS, not instead of it. We integrate with Guidewire via API. You get AI-native document intelligence without disrupting your core migration." |
| "How is this different from what Microsoft Copilot does?" | "Copilot is horizontal — it knows everything about nothing in insurance. Charterwell is purpose-built for claims: it understands policy language, coverage terms, medical codes, and regulatory requirements that a general-purpose AI never will." |
| "I need SOC 2 and a security review before we can even start a pilot." | "We're SOC 2 Type II compliant. We'll share our security documentation upfront and participate in your vendor security assessment process." |

## Persona 4: The Gatekeeper

### Chief Compliance Officer / Chief Risk Officer

**Demographics:** 48-60 years old. Legal or regulatory background. Often a former state insurance regulator or compliance attorney. Deeply risk-averse by nature and by professional mandate. Reports to CEO or General Counsel.

---

### Why This Persona Can Kill a Deal

The CCO doesn't buy software. They veto it. In a regulated industry like insurance, a compliance objection can delay or permanently block a purchase decision. The CCO's concerns are legitimate and must be proactively addressed — not dismissed as "FUD."

---

### Top Concerns in 2026

**1. Regulatory Risk: The NAIC Model Bulletin and State-Level Action**

The NAIC's Model Bulletin on AI is now state law in nearly half the country. Colorado, Virginia, Connecticut, and Pennsylvania have enacted or are advancing specialized AI legislation for insurance. Regulators are expected to start using the AI Systems Evaluation Tool during market conduct examinations in 2026. A draft model law on third-party data and models is anticipated.

**What this means for Charterwell:** Any AI used in claims decisions must be auditable, explainable, and compliant with a patchwork of state-level regulations. The CCO needs to know: "If a regulator examines how we used AI on a specific claim, can we show them exactly what the AI did and why?"

**2. Explainability of AI Decisions**

Regulators are demanding transparency — the ability to explain how AI inputs lead to specific outputs or decisions. In insurance specifically, if an AI system influences claim valuation, coverage determination, or fraud flagging, the carrier must be able to explain that decision to the claimant, the regulator, and potentially a court.

The compliance standard: "If the data and logic cannot be explained, the system should not be deployed."

**3. Audit Trails and Documentation**

Insurers must maintain clear documentation of automated decisions and demonstrate human oversight for critical business processes. AI governance is no longer a technical concern — regulators expect executive leadership and boards to understand how AI is used.

**4. Bias and Fairness**

AI systems that inadvertently discriminate based on protected characteristics create existential regulatory risk. The CCO needs assurance that the AI has been tested for disparate impact and that ongoing monitoring is in place.

**5. Third-Party AI Risk**

Using a vendor's AI system doesn't transfer regulatory liability. The carrier is still responsible for decisions made using third-party AI. The CCO needs to understand: what data does Charterwell's AI use? Where is it stored? Who has access? Can it be audited?

---

### How We Preemptively Address These Concerns

| Concern | Charterwell's Response |
|---|---|
| **"Can we explain what the AI did on a specific claim?"** | Every AI action produces an audit trail: input document, extracted data, confidence scores, applied rules, and recommended action. Full decision lineage is exportable for regulatory examination. |
| **"Does this comply with NAIC Model Bulletin requirements?"** | Charterwell is designed for NAIC compliance: human-in-the-loop for material claim decisions, documented AI governance framework, regular model performance reporting. |
| **"What about state-specific regulations?"** | Our compliance module is configurable by jurisdiction. As new state laws take effect, we update compliance rules — you don't have to track 50 states yourself. |
| **"How do we know the AI isn't biased?"** | Bias testing is part of our model validation process. We provide disparate impact analysis reports and continuous fairness monitoring dashboards. |
| **"Where is our data?"** | Tenant-isolated data architecture. Configurable data residency. No cross-tenant data sharing. Full data deletion available on contract termination. Your data never trains models used by other customers. |
| **"Who is responsible when the AI is wrong?"** | Charterwell augments human decision-making — it doesn't replace it. Every material claim decision requires human approval. The AI recommends; the adjuster decides. |

---

### What the CCO Needs to See Before Approval

1. **AI Governance Documentation** — Written framework covering model oversight, testing, monitoring, and incident response
2. **Regulatory Mapping** — How Charterwell maps to NAIC Model Bulletin requirements, state-level AI laws, and applicable data privacy regulations
3. **Audit Trail Demo** — Live walkthrough showing the complete decision trail for a sample claim
4. **Security and Privacy Assessment** — SOC 2 report, data processing agreement, privacy impact assessment
5. **Bias Testing Results** — Disparate impact analysis across protected characteristics
6. **Human Oversight Architecture** — Documentation of where and how human review is required in the workflow

---

## The Buying Committee Dynamic

### How These Personas Interact in a Deal

```
Champion discovers Charterwell
        ↓
Champion advocates to VP Claims (Operator)
        ↓
VP Claims requests pilot budget from CIO (Architect)
        ↓
CIO approves pilot → assigns architecture review
        ↓
Pilot runs (60-90 days, Champion drives adoption)
        ↓
Pilot succeeds → VP Claims proposes enterprise rollout
        ↓
CIO evaluates TCO, integration, scalability
        ↓
CCO (Gatekeeper) conducts compliance review
        ↓
Enterprise deal closes
```

**Typical deal timeline:** 6-12 months from first contact to enterprise contract.

**Critical handoff points:**
- Champion → Operator: Must have business case, not just product enthusiasm
- Operator → Architect: Must speak to technology strategy, not just claims pain
- Architect → Gatekeeper: Must have governance documentation ready *before* the CCO asks
