## 1. Brand Voice

### Voice Positioning: McKinsey Meets Stripe

The brief says it well: **professional, intelligent, confident — we speak the language of insurance, not Silicon Valley.** This is the right instinct. Insurance buyers are conservative, risk-averse, and allergic to hype. Our voice must earn trust before it earns excitement.

### Voice Principles

| Principle | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Authoritative, not arrogant** | We know insurance. We don't lecture about it. We demonstrate mastery through specificity, not superlatives. | "Claims cycle time drops 40% when document triage is automated" — not "Our revolutionary AI will transform your business." |
| **Precise, not jargon-heavy** | Use insurance terminology correctly (FNOL, LAE, combined ratio) but don't hide behind it. Every sentence should be understood by a VP Claims and a board member. | "Every document understood, every decision informed" — not "Our NLP pipeline delivers state-of-the-art entity extraction." |
| **Confident, not aggressive** | We state what we do. We don't attack competitors by name in public materials. We let the differentiation speak. | "One workspace replaces your claims AI stack" — not "Unlike [competitor], we actually work." |
| **Human, not corporate** | Insurance is a people business. Claims affect real lives. Our voice acknowledges the human stakes without being sentimental. | "Your examiners deserve better than alt-tabbing between 12 windows" — not "Optimize human capital allocation in claims workflows." |
| **Specific, not vague** | Numbers, timelines, outcomes. No "best-in-class," no "end-to-end," no "leverage." | "15-20 minutes per FNOL, reduced to under 3" — not "Dramatically faster claims processing." |

### Voice Quick-Check

Before publishing any external content, apply this filter:

1. **Would a VP of Claims at a $5B carrier take this seriously?** If it sounds like a startup pitch deck, rewrite.
2. **Does it use our language system?** (See 01-category-creation.md, Section 6.) Workspace, not platform. Claims professionals, not users. AI-native, not AI-powered. Understand, not process.
3. **Is there a number or a specific outcome?** Generalities erode credibility. If we can't be specific, we shouldn't say it yet.
4. **Would we say this in a room with Legora's founders?** The bar is: peers who built a $5.5B vertical AI company would nod, not cringe.

### Voice by Channel

| Channel | Register | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Website** | Authoritative, concise | Lead with outcomes. Short sentences. No filler. Think Stripe's homepage density. |
| **Pitch deck** | Narrative, confident | Tell the story (problem → insight → product → why us). Use the strategic narrative arc. |
| **Sales conversations** | Consultative, specific | Mirror the buyer's language. Use their KPIs. Ask questions before making claims. |
| **Conference talks** | Thought leadership, visionary | Speak about the category, not just the product. Educate the audience on the shift happening. |
| **LinkedIn / social** | Sharp, opinionated | Take positions. "Point solutions are the new legacy." Provoke discussion, don't post platitudes. |
| **Technical docs** | Clear, precise | No marketing language. Accuracy over style. |
| **Compliance materials** | Formal, thorough | Match the register of the regulatory audience. No shortcuts. |

## 2. Visual Identity Direction

### Design Philosophy: Trust at Speed

Insurance buyers need to feel **safety and reliability**, but Charterwell is a modern AI company, not a legacy insurer. The visual identity must balance these tensions:

| Tension | Resolution |
|---|---|
| Innovation vs. trust | Modern design with muted confidence — not flashy startup, not stodgy incumbent |
| Technology vs. humanity | Clean interfaces that feel warm, not clinical. The product is for people, not machines. |
| Complexity vs. clarity | Insurance workflows are complex. The brand must feel simple, organized, competent. |

### Color System

| Token | Hex | Role | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary: Deep Navy** | `#1B2A4A` | Headlines, primary UI surfaces, logo | Signals authority, trust, intelligence. Universal in financial services without being generic. |
| **Accent: Teal** | `#0D9488` | CTAs, links, active states, highlights | Modern, distinctive, high contrast against navy. Differentiates from the sea of blue fintech brands. |
| **Surface: Warm White** | `#FAFAF8` | Backgrounds, cards | Slightly warm to avoid clinical feel. Not pure white. |
| **Text: Charcoal** | `#1F2937` | Body copy | High readability, softer than pure black. |
| **Success: Emerald** | `#059669` | Positive states, confirmations | Clear, accessible green. |
| **Warning: Amber** | `#D97706` | Alerts, attention states | Visible without alarm. |
| **Error: Crimson** | `#DC2626` | Errors, critical alerts | Standard. |
| **Muted: Slate** | `#64748B` | Secondary text, borders, disabled states | Quiet, professional. |

### Typography

| Use | Font | Weight | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Headlines** | Inter | 600 (Semibold) | Clean geometric sans-serif. Professional but modern. Widely supported. |
| **Body** | Inter | 400 (Regular) | Highly legible at small sizes. Excellent for data-dense interfaces. |
| **Code / Data** | JetBrains Mono | 400 | For technical docs, API references, data displays. |
| **Marketing display** | Inter | 700 (Bold) or a serif option for manifesto-style content | Consider a serif (e.g., Playfair Display) for whitepaper titles and thought leadership — adds gravitas. |

### Logo Direction

| Guideline | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Mark** | Wordmark-first approach. "Charterwell" in custom-spaced Inter Semibold or a bespoke typeface. Optional: a simple geometric icon (abstract document layers, or a "C" monogram) for favicon and social use. |
| **Color** | Deep Navy on light backgrounds. White on dark backgrounds. Single-color only — **no gradients**. |
| **Clearspace** | Minimum clearspace = height of the "C" on all sides. |
| **Minimum size** | 100px wide for digital. 25mm for print. |
| **No-gos** | No gradients. No drop shadows. No 3D effects. No icons that look like generic AI brains or neural networks. |

### Iconography

- **Style:** Outlined, 1.5px stroke weight, consistent with Inter's geometry.
- **Theme:** Document-centric metaphors — pages, layers, stacks, checkmarks, routing arrows.
- **Avoid:** Robot/AI iconography (brains, neural networks, circuit boards). We are a workspace, not a science experiment.

### Photography & Illustration Direction

- **People:** Real insurance professionals in real work environments. No stock-photo diversity panels. If we don't have real photos, use no photos rather than fake ones.
- **Abstract:** Layered, organized geometric compositions that evoke structured documents. Think blueprint aesthetics — precision without coldness.
- **Avoid:** Futuristic/sci-fi imagery, floating holograms, robots shaking hands, anything that signals "AI startup" over "insurance technology partner."

## 3. Naming Validation: "Charterwell"

### Trademark Landscape

| Entity | Name | Industry | Geography | Conflict Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Chartwell Insurance Services** | Chartwell | Insurance brokerage (personal property, liability) | U.S. (Chicago) — now part of EPIC Insurance Brokers | **HIGH** — one letter difference ("Charterwell" vs. "Chartwell"), same industry. Potential for buyer confusion. |
| **Chartwell Insurance Brokers** | Chartwell | Insurance brokerage | UK | **HIGH** — same name confusion risk in UK market. |
| **Chartwell Compliance** | Chartwell | Regulatory compliance for financial services | U.S. | **MODERATE** — adjacent industry, same name confusion. |
| **Charterwells Accountants** | Charterwells | Accounting, payroll, tax | UK (Stanmore) | **LOW** — different industry, different geography, plural form. |
| **Charterwell Maritime SA** | Charterwell | Shipping / vessel management | Greece (Athens) | **LOW** — different industry, different geography, exact name match but unrelated. |
| **Charterwell Limited** | Charterwell | UK registered company | UK | **LOW-MODERATE** — depends on active status and industry. Requires Companies House check. |

### Domain Status

| Domain | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **charterwell.ai** | Project is named this — presumably owned or targeted | Premium .ai domain. Ideal for category positioning. |
| **charterwell.com** | Unknown — requires WHOIS check | Should be acquired if available. Even if .ai is primary, .com provides credibility for enterprise buyers. |
| **chartwell.com** | Likely taken (multiple Chartwell entities exist) | Not available. |

### Cultural & Linguistic Associations

**Positive:**
- "Charter" evokes founding documents, authority, rights, legitimacy. A charter is a grant of rights — fitting for a company that grants claims professionals new capabilities.
- "Well" evokes a source, abundance, health. "All is well." Comforting in insurance context.
- Combined: "Charterwell" suggests a source of authority, a well of established knowledge.
- Historical echo: Chartwell was Winston Churchill's country home — associations with leadership, resilience, British gravitas. The extra "r" in "Charterwell" creates distance while retaining the echo.

**Negative / Risks:**
- Phonetic confusion with "Chartwell" is inevitable. In phone conversations, emails, and word-of-mouth, people will drop the "r" and search for "Chartwell" instead.
- **The Chartwell Insurance Services overlap is the #1 naming risk.** A VP Claims googling "Chartwell AI insurance" will find Chartwell Insurance Services first. This is a real GTM friction point.

### Naming Recommendation

| Option | Verdict |
|---|---|
| **Keep "Charterwell"** | Viable if: (a) trademark counsel confirms no Class 9/42 conflict with Chartwell entities, (b) we invest heavily in SEO to own "Charterwell" search results, and (c) we always use the full name (never shorten to "Charter"). The name has strong brand qualities and the .ai domain is distinctive. |
| **Consider alternatives** | Only if trademark counsel identifies a blocking conflict. The name is good — the question is whether the Chartwell proximity creates legal or market confusion that outweighs the brand value. |

### Required Next Steps (Before Public Launch)

1. **Trademark search (formal):** Engage IP counsel to run a comprehensive trademark search for "Charterwell" in USPTO Classes 9 (software), 35 (business services), 36 (insurance services), and 42 (SaaS/technology services). Check EU, UK, and international registers.
2. **Chartwell conflict assessment:** Specifically analyze whether "Charterwell" creates likelihood of confusion with "Chartwell Insurance Services" under the DuPont factors (similarity of marks, relatedness of services, trade channels, sophistication of buyers).
3. **Domain acquisition:** Confirm ownership of charterwell.ai. Attempt to acquire charterwell.com. Register charterwell.io, charterwell.co as defensive registrations.
4. **Companies House check:** Verify the status and nature of Charterwell Limited (UK company number 07469572).

## 4. Competitive Battle Cards

### How to Use These Cards

Each battle card is a one-page reference for sales conversations. When a prospect mentions a competitor, pull the relevant card. The structure is:
- **Who they are** (one line)
- **What they do well** (concede strengths — builds credibility)
- **Where they fall short** (relative to Charterwell's value prop)
- **Our counter-position** (the line to use)
- **Proof points** (specific data or examples)
- **Trap questions** (questions to ask that expose the competitor's weakness)

---

### Battle Card: Tractable

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Computer vision for visual damage appraisal. $185M raised, $1B valuation. |
| **Strengths** | Best-in-class auto/property damage assessment from photos. Strong carrier relationships (Tokio Marine, The Hartford, Ageas). Proven at scale in 10+ countries. |
| **Gaps** | Photos only — no document intelligence. Cannot process policy text, medical records, compliance filings, adjuster notes, or any non-image content. Single use case in a multi-step workflow. |
| **Counter-position** | *"Tractable reads photos. Charterwell reads everything else. A claim isn't a photo — it's 10-15 documents. We start where Tractable stops."* |
| **Proof points** | Average P&C claim touches 10-15 documents; only 1-2 are images. Tractable covers <15% of the document surface area of a claim. |
| **Trap questions** | "How do you handle the police report, medical records, and policy declaration page on this claim?" / "What happens after the damage estimate — who routes the claim, checks coverage, and ensures compliance?" |
| **Partnership angle** | Tractable is a natural integration partner. Their damage scores feed into Charterwell's workspace. Position as complementary, not competitive. |

---

### Battle Card: Shift Technology

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | AI for fraud detection → expanding to claims automation. $320M raised, $1B valuation. Being acquired by Predictiv AI. |
| **Strengths** | Deep fraud detection expertise. 400+ insurer relationships. AXA 5-year renewal (March 2026). Launched "Shift Claims" (Sept 2025) — agentic AI for claims triage with strong early metrics (3% claims loss reduction, 30% faster handling, 60% automation). |
| **Gaps** | DNA is analytics and decisioning, not document intelligence. Shift Claims automates triage and routing — it does not rebuild the document layer. No workspace concept. The Predictiv AI acquisition creates strategic uncertainty. |
| **Counter-position** | *"Shift tells you whether a claim is suspicious. Charterwell processes the documents that make up the claim. We're the intelligence layer; they're the decision layer. Different problems."* |
| **Proof points** | Shift's "document analysis" is a feature, not a platform. Their core product was built for fraud — claims automation is a lateral expansion, not their foundation. Acquisition by Predictiv AI (sub-$1B outcome on $320M raised) signals strategic challenges. |
| **Trap questions** | "Can Shift process a 200-page medical record and extract the relevant diagnoses for a bodily injury claim?" / "How does Shift handle your state-specific compliance filing requirements?" / "What happens to the Shift roadmap under Predictiv AI ownership?" |
| **Risk** | Most dangerous near-term competitor. If Shift builds a real document layer, they have the GTM (400+ carriers) to win. **Our window to establish the document workspace before Shift gets there is 18-24 months.** |

---

### Battle Card: Hyperscience

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Horizontal Intelligent Document Processing (IDP). $300M+ raised. Claims 99.5% extraction accuracy. |
| **Strengths** | Proven extraction accuracy. Enterprise customers (AmEx, Schwab, SSA, VA). Strong technical foundation. Insurance case study: 85% processing time reduction for a top-10 insurer. |
| **Gaps** | Horizontal — serves insurance, government, financial services with the same platform. No insurance-specific intelligence: doesn't understand coverage types, adjudication rules, state-specific compliance, or claims workflows. It's a tool, not a workspace. |
| **Counter-position** | *"Hyperscience extracts data from documents. Charterwell understands what that data means for your claim. Extraction is step one — coverage determination, adjuster routing, and compliance checking are steps two through ten."* |
| **Proof points** | IDP accuracy is table stakes — the value is in the insurance intelligence layer on top. A generic IDP cannot determine coverage applicability, route to the right adjuster by claim type, or check 50-state compliance requirements. |
| **Trap questions** | "Can Hyperscience determine whether a claim falls within policy coverage based on the extracted data?" / "How does it handle state-specific compliance requirements for claims in Pennsylvania vs. California?" / "Does your Hyperscience deployment route claims to the right adjuster, or does your team still do that manually?" |

### Battle Card: FurtherAI

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | AI assistant for insurance workflows. $30M raised (a16z). YC-backed. Processing billions in premiums. |
| **Strengths** | Strong investor backing (Andreessen Horowitz). Rapid growth. Broad insurance workflow coverage (submissions, underwriting, claims, compliance). YC network and credibility. |
| **Gaps** | Going wide (many workflows) rather than deep (document intelligence as foundation). Breadth without depth is vulnerable to specialists. No visible playbook system or carrier-specific customization moat. Early-stage — fewer production deployments and reference customers than incumbents. |
| **Counter-position** | *"FurtherAI is building an AI assistant for many insurance tasks. Charterwell is building the workspace for claims professionals. We go deep on the document layer — the foundation that every claims workflow depends on."* |
| **Proof points** | Depth vs. breadth: Charterwell's document intelligence engine is purpose-built for the 50 most common P&C document types with insurance-specific extraction. FurtherAI's generalist approach means shallower understanding of each domain. |
| **Trap questions** | "Can you show me how FurtherAI handles a complex bodily injury claim with 50+ pages of medical records?" / "How does FurtherAI's document extraction accuracy compare on insurance-specific documents vs. generic PDFs?" / "What carrier-specific customization is available?" |
| **Risk** | **Primary competitive threat.** a16z backing gives them resources and distribution. If they deepen their document layer before we establish market presence, the differentiation narrows. Speed to market is critical. |

### Battle Card: Guidewire / Duck Creek (Legacy Incumbents)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Core P&C insurance platforms. Guidewire: $1.2B revenue, 19% ARR growth. Duck Creek: Vista Equity-owned, "90-day implementation" positioning. |
| **Strengths** | Massive installed base — Guidewire alone has hundreds of carriers. Deep integration into carrier operations. Multi-year contracts create lock-in. Adding AI capabilities (Guidewire AI, Duck Creek AI). Liberty Mutual signed a 10-year cloud deal with Guidewire. |
| **Gaps** | Legacy architecture with AI bolted on, not built in. Implementations cost millions and take months-to-years. AI capabilities are features, not the foundation. They manage claim *records* — they don't understand claim *documents*. |
| **Counter-position** | *"Guidewire is your system of record. Charterwell is your system of intelligence. We don't replace your core — we make it smarter. Every document that flows into Guidewire passes through Charterwell first."* |
| **Proof points** | Guidewire/Duck Creek implementations: $5-50M+, 12-24 months. Charterwell pilot: defined scope, 60-90 days, measurable ROI before enterprise commitment. We sit alongside the core, not against it. |
| **Trap questions** | "How long did your last Guidewire enhancement take to deploy?" / "Can Guidewire's AI process a handwritten police report or extract medical codes from a 200-page record?" / "How many AI tools are you running alongside Guidewire today? How well do they integrate with each other?" |
| **Partnership angle** | Guidewire integration is a Day 1 priority. Position Charterwell as the document intelligence layer that *enhances* Guidewire — every Guidewire carrier is a prospect. |

### Battle Card: "We'll Build It In-House"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | Carrier's internal engineering / innovation team. |
| **Strengths** | Full control over data, architecture, and customization. No vendor dependency. Internal champions who understand the carrier's specific workflows. |
| **Gaps** | 88% of carriers want to build AI. Only 7% have scaled it into production. Insurance AI requires specialized document understanding, 50-state compliance knowledge, and continuous model improvement — capabilities that take years and millions to develop internally. |
| **Counter-position** | *"88% want to build. 7% succeed. The gap isn't ambition — it's the specialized AI expertise that insurance documents demand. Why spend two years and $10M building what Charterwell delivers in 90 days?"* |
| **Proof points** | Internal AI projects: avg. 18-24 months to production, high failure rate. Charterwell pilot: 60-90 days to measurable results. The document intelligence engine already understands 50+ P&C document types — that's years of specialized training your team would need to replicate. |
| **Trap questions** | "How many of your AI pilots have made it to production?" / "What's your timeline for processing all document types across all claim types?" / "Who on your team has built insurance-specific document intelligence models before?" |
